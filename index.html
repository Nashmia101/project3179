Average Daily Rainfall in Peninsular Malaysia

Average Rainfall trends in Peninsular Malaysia

Peninsular Malaysia is rainy, but not evenly so. The east coast gets the most rain each year. Terengganu is often highest, around 4–5 mm a day. Perlis is usually lowest, near 1 mm a day. Most other states sit between 2–3 mm a day. This follows the late-year Northeast Monsoon.

Monthly Rainfall Trends in Peninsular Malaysia (2014–2020)

Rainfall patterns across months

Rainfall in Peninsular Malaysia follows a clear seasonal cycle, with low rainfall from February to August and a sharp rise from October to December during the Northeast Monsoon. The east coast, especially Kelantan, Terengganu, and Pahang, receives the heaviest rain, often exceeding 10–20 mm per day. Western states like Perlis, Penang, and Melaka stay drier, while Selangor, Negeri Sembilan, and Johor see moderate year-end increases. The monsoon remains the main driver of Malaysia’s recurring wet and dry seasons.

State wise Mean Temperature Trends in Peninsular Malaysia (2014–2020)

Mean Minimum and Maximum Temperature across states.

From 2014 to 2020, Perlis, Selangor, and Perak record the highest maximum temperatures around 33–34 °C, while Pahang and Terengganu remain cooler at 30–31 °C because of heavier rainfall and cloud cover.

For minimum temperatures, Selangor-Wilayah, Penang, and Melaka stay warmer at night (24–25 °C) due to drier conditions and urban heat, while Pahang is the coolest (19–22 °C) from rain and elevation.

Overall, temperatures stay steady with slightly warmer nights, showing that higher rainfall keeps states cooler.

Humidity Bands by Station (2014–2020)

Variations in Humidity across Peninsular Malaysia

From 2014 to 2020, most weather stations in Peninsular Malaysia showed very high humidity of 80 percent or more. The share was highest in 2017 at 85 percent and lowest in 2016 and 2019 at 59 percent. It went up a little again in 2020 to 67 percent.

This agrees with your summary that the highlands and east coast stay very humid most of the time, while some urban and west coast stations drop slightly to the 75–80 percent range. The data also fits the rainfall pattern since years with higher humidity usually have more wet days during the monsoon months.

Water Production by State and Year (2014–2020)

Water production across years in Peninsular Malaysia.

From 2014 to 2020, Selangor recorded the highest water production, rising from about 4,594 to 4,967 million litres, followed by Johor and Kedah, while Perlis remained the lowest, below 300 million litres.

This pattern relates to rainfall and population, as states with steady rainfall and higher demand, like Selangor and Johor, produce more water. In contrast, states with fewer facilities and lower demand, such as Perlis and Kelantan, record lower production.

Average Rainfall vs Water Production (2014–2020)

Relationship between Average Rainfall and Water production

From 2014 to 2020, Selangor had the highest water production despite moderate rainfall, while Terengganu and Kelantan showed high rainfall but lower production. Perlis remained the lowest. Overall, the charts show a weak negative link between rainfall and production, with population and demand being the main factors.
